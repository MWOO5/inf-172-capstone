Research on Sleep & Music

Why sleep matters
for students

As a team, our project focuses on the growing rates of insomnia and poor sleep among young adult university students. For many students, university is the first time they are navigating a completely new routine, new living environments, heavier academic workloads, jobs, shifting responsibilities, and an expanded social life. While these experiences can be exciting, they also make it easy to fall into irregular sleep patterns (late nights, inconsistent bedtimes, and poor sleep hygiene), which quickly accumulate to low-quality sleep.

Addressing this need is paramount because sleep is not just ‚Äúrest,‚Äù it shapes how students function day to day. Inadequate sleep among university students has been linked to a higher risk of depressive symptoms[1]. Poor sleep is also associated with reduced cognitive performance [2], including weaker memory, impaired concentration, and mood disturbances [3]. Over time, that drop in cognitive performance can undermine academic outcomes, increase stress, and spill into other areas of life. Without a practical intervention, insomnia and poor sleep can affect students physically, mentally, socially, and emotionally.
